LEGO MOCs for Gifts

2/26/2017

1 Comment

 
    Two years or so ago I figured it out. I found an easy(ish) way to always have a nice gift ready for anyone around the holidays or as a birthday present. The answer, at least for us builders, is LEGO! Using your creativity you can design something small yet neat and then order the parts to make a solid handful of your MOC for all your friends and family.
    Of course depending on who the gift may be for some small creation may not suffice, but for those estranged aunts and uncles it’ll do the job well. For the last three Christmases I’ve designed a holiday related LEGO ornament and gifted it to all the folks closest to me. After finishing the model, I turn to Bricklink for the parts to make five to ten, mostly depending on how hard the parts are to come by.
    The first year was these snowmen. I actually still really like this model, the techniques in it are particularly compact. Plus all the parts are pretty common and cheap.
    The next year’s was inspired by Katie Walker on Flickr, who had some beautiful, little mosaics of holiday images that I basically encased in glass and put a hook on. These looked really cool, and I even made a video, but all the transparent colored cheese slopes were on the expensive side.
    And last year, 2016, I took some inspiration from Chris McVeigh’s Santa ornament and made my own, which in the end looks quite a bit different. This final design I was super happy with. It was easy to make, relatively cheap, looked nice, and for my own amusement, had some creative parts usage.
    But before gifting a LEGO model, whether it be an ornament or a friend’s dog, there are some preparations to be made. Usually the pieces I’m building them out of are new from Bricklink and I just keep the original design for myself, but if the parts are coming from your bins and some may be discolored or dirty, I would wash them or even soak them in hydrogen peroxide. Then, a step some readers may absolutely despise: glue them. I understand you may not want your LEGO glued, but the person on the receiving end would love the security of knowing their gift won’t break from any fall or mishandling.
I think this is a must. Go out, buy a small bottle of Krazy Glue and take the few minutes to glue the builds. Although the glue is supposed to be invisible, it really shows up on the shiny LEGO parts, so I would recommend disassembling a little to try and lather glue where it can’t be seen, but will get the job done.
Anyway, I hope this helps you in your endeavors to find gifts for all those great people in your life. If you have any questions leave them in the comments below. Thanks for reading.

Plastic to Hold More Plastic

2/19/2017

0 Comments

 
   Prior we discussed how to actually sort all your plastic bricks lying about in an attempt to make them logically organized; now, we can move on to options for actually storing them. All the different methods’ pros and cons that I at least am aware of and have used.
   Which method you use essentially comes down to two things: how much you want to spend on storage and the state of your collection. The state of your collection in this case being how much you have of in the different categories, and how much you want to sort it out.
   You basically need to get storage accordingly to how you’re going to sort your collection. Not only have I sorted certain parts of my collection out heavily, I've got a lot of things from Pick a Brick or Bricklink where there's individual parts in decent quantities, and I don't want to throw them in one of the big general bins. So I need some smaller drawers to put those in, and usually a lot of them. But I also have some categories that are much larger, like my dark grey slopes, thus I need some bigger drawers for those. Then of course all the ones in between.
   You may have come to this conclusion through what I just said, but what I would recommend if a variety of storage compartment sizes. How much you get of the different sizes, like these big ones, medium ones, and really small ones, is totally down to your collection and how many parts you have and how much you’re sorting it out.
   There are some different methods of storage that I'm going to share with you. The first being what I consider the best and most convenient: drawers. Now they are also probably the most expensive, but I think it’s worth it. The cheaper methods could include plastic bags. These are a big hassle most of the time, if you do choose these I would recommend tacking them to a board so you can see all of them, like Kris Productions. Another is toates, which can be found at the dollar store. These are usually a hassle, but I still have a few around for extra storage of whatever. One last thing to consider as part of your storage solution is PAB cups if you get those a lot, they can be really helpful for single things you have a lot of.
   I get asked all the time “What should I sort my parts in, or where do I you get your drawers” and such. There really isn’t one single place to go get drawers from. Most of the time it’s just down to the situation. If you see a good deal, get those. I’m sure lots of places around you sell different kinds of drawers. I got these bigger white ones from Target, and the stack-ons at Lowes. These I got for a dollar each at a closing hallmark, and these were at a yard sale. I even use shoe boxes for some things.
   In the end it just comes down to a lot of variety in your choices for drawers, and how much you get of the different types comes down to how much of the different categories you have and how much you’re going to sort.
   If someone is looking for a place to store their parts, redirect them here. If you’ve got anything to add, let me know down in the comments so anyone else browsing the article will see also. Thanks for reading, hope it was worth it!

﻿Discouraged Over Broken Bricks

2/12/2017

0 Comments

 
    For weeks now I’ve been on and off making tiny bits of progress on a LEGO project due within the next few unspecific weeks. If you can’t already tell, I’m not allowed to talk about what exactly the build is just yet. Hopefully at some point you will find out. Anyway, the fact that I can’t find any inspiration to work some something seemingly simple isn’t what I’m writing about. Last weekend I tried getting the wheels turning on this MOC, but almost as soon as I begun I ran square into a huge roadblock.
   One of the most familiar building techniques to almost all builders is one that is actually considered “illegal” by LEGO because of how it stresses the bricks, so they won’t use it in any sets or products meant to last. Despite that thousands of builders have made curved walls out of staggered 1x2 bricks, which have just enough leeway to flex and essentially create curves out of bricks.
    I’ve done this many times myself with a variety of different bricks. You can only bend/flex the 1x2 bricks so far before the wall of them basically just snaps and you have to reconnect it and try again. The build I’m working on is predominantly dark red and requires this curving technique for some of the walls, out of dark red. I had some dark red 1x2’s back from Pick-A-Brick over 5 years ago. I began constructing a wall out of them, and once I had a decent length I went on to bend it to my desired radius. Which, keep in mind, isn’t actually a very tight curve, especially compared to what I, and others, have achieved. Upon arching it a reasonable distance, just about where I need it, I heard some strange cracks and creaks coming from the bricks. Different than normal. Although I knew something wasn’t right I cared on and bent it slightly further, knowing that the worst that could happen is it snaps apart and I put it back together.
    Instead, at one single point, the wall completely snapped. Once I picked up the remnants it was clear what I had gone wrong: multiple pieces in that one spot had broken under the minimal stress. This is very strange for LEGO as quality has always been a priority, and bricks without an easy breaking point can’t be easily “snapped.” Continuing to experiment I reconnected the 1x2 bricks and tried again: same exact result. Throughout this process I actually ended up damaging a plethora more of a bricks that simply didn’t collapse the entire wall with them.
    My frustration reminded me of other times LEGO pieces had failed me, say under the pressure of a brick separator or whatever. But for some reason I couldn’t stop thinking there was an anomaly in the frequency of broken dark red pieces in the past, even though it’s nowhere near my most used color. Since I’m always looking to spark a discussion I took to social media asking if anyone else had experienced the same thing. On both Flickr and Instagram I got the same responses, which matched up with my story. Quite a few comments also mentioned the same problem with reddish brown, a very similar looking color.
    I went to Google looking for any forum thread or post with any mention of the issue but came up mostly dry. The only relevant article is from Eurobricks user CP5670, showing when certain LEGO colors were changed. A reply from Svelte states that some time in 2009 the dark red color was changed to be more consistent from batch to batch, which resulted in it having a noticeably more brown tinge, the only other reported color to be having issues.
    At this point it sounds like I’m trying to start a conspiracy theory about brown-ish dark red bricks and reddish-brown bricks and how they’re the only ones known to be so brittle under pressure. Since then the problem was probably noticed by LEGO and fixed, proven by Anders from AlCloneProductions who decided to try it for himself with his dark red bricks, completing an incredible curve without them breaking or even snapping apart. As Sean Edmison puts it on my Flickr post, “Let this stand as a warning to anyone who thinks that all bricks are created equal.”
