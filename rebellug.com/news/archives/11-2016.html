A Word on Mass Sorting

11/28/2016

1 Comment

 
Throughout winter break I’ve been sorting some collections I sort of absorbed and MOCs I finished. I wanted to make something out of all this lego sorting and that gave me enough inspiration to do this.

But that’s not the point. I have a few words that I want to share on mass sorting brick. Without getting a Javier or involving local children, how do you remain sane while sorting thousands of tiny plastic parts into a few hundred drawers and totes. And are there any ways to cheat the system and do it faster? We’ll get to that later.

Me, personally, I listen to music audiobooks or movies, sometimes eavesdrop on IDS wannabes. Basically anything that doesn’t require my full attention and keeps me entertained.

But how do I pick up efficiency? I think that’s what everyone's actually here for. Taking breaks and getting out of the groove slows you down a lot. A goal I had with this huge batch of sorting was to try and improve my attention span because normally I would stop every hour and go play rocket league until I lost and the only way to cope with my self loath was torture in the form of lego sorting, yet again

Another big one is to physically surround yourself with all the drawers you need or find yourself going to all the time. One time I surrounded myself with actual drawer units as if it was a fort for some sort of satanic ritual. But it worked, and I could get a ton done in one day. Just had to deal with the pain of crossing your legs for 10 hours at a time since I was sitting on the floor.
But the big thing here is to just temporarily get all the final destinations of parts as close as possible

Finally, I suggest going batch by batch. The logic here is to be sorting similar pieces all at one time. That will limit traveling time and how much you’re picking a particular thing out. Big time saver. After my boy KrisProductions made his collection video that you never got to see, he destroyed all his sets in spectacular fashion down the stairs. Although he filmed it, apparently that never got anywhere either. But that’s besides the point. He warned me about destroying sets and combining all of them in a big sorting pile because he ended up with way more variety than he needed. If he went set by set, batch by batch, this would have been a lot easier. This should also be applied to MOCs. Just do one MOC at a time.
This idea obviously doesn't work if it’s all in a giant concoction of bricks, but you still get those little achievements along the way.

On a second note, putting it in a low, yet large ish tub will allow you to see most of what you’re going through at one time. This makes it easy to pick out all of one piece so you can slowly take out different categories. From my experience this is the fastest way, but I do also occasionally just take a big handful and walk around and sort out each part into its respective drawer.

But yeah, I’ll take Kris’ advice and use it over the next few weeks as I sort and sort the collections I’ve absorbed and the MOCs I forgot to part out. But once I’m done I have some real cool plans. That’s usually what drives me, the next project. Stay tuned to all the RebelLUG channels for not only my content, but the rest of the groups’, because we all go through these same engless struggles. Anyway, thank you for reading. Your feedback is just as appreciated if you like the blog or not, as long as it’s honest.

Taking it to the Extreme

11/14/2016

0 Comments

 
                Kris, straight out of the Kris Production Studio, is a veteran of the LEGO YouTube community. He’s been on the scene since June of 2009. Over the years he’s become one of the most well-known Star Wars builders, with over 24,000 subscribers.
                These blogs are here to help you, the readers, find out more about different styles and techniques some of the RebelLUG guys do that you may find interesting. Everyone has their own little, or in this case, big distinguishing X factor; essentially what makes them unique as a LEGO builder. Kris found that in his passion for huge dioramas. It soon became what he strived for in each project and what he became known for on the Internet. Scrolling through his history of 129 videos, it’s no secret that’s been his goal from the beginning: go bigger.
                If you’re in the minority and haven’t seen some of the MOCs I’m referencing, here are some of the best examples: ​
Massive displays, especially ones still filled with details, never cease to amaze. Based on just a quick glance at the comments on these videos, there is clearly an audience of people awaiting answers for tips on how to build such massive LEGO models. So I asked Kris for just that.

Before trying to make larger MOCs, what should aspiring builders try to master first?

“I may be known for the scale or size of my creations, but I really just like to grasp a larger picture of a build. Its like the difference between painting on a small canvas or using a whole wall. I really like to capture a larger picture, something that cannot be fairly represented on a small scale. Other times I really just go for size accuracy.
Artistically speaking, know what you are building and how you want the final outcome to look. Realistically speaking, know the maximum capabilities of your lego collection are, so you know just what you can build out of what you have.”

How do you conserve pieces on something so massive?

“Knowing when to use pieces is a critical part of building large scale. Know what you need to use for the whole build, so you don't use up a colour on unseen parts of the build that are required later. Or vice versa to use the un-usable colours where they will not be seen and get those good looking ones into the sun! Really know the whole project. Goes hand in hand with knowing your pieces. “
What are some tips for accumulating pieces? Obviously you didn’t buy everything you have now at once.

Do you use any useful strategies when looking to buy more bricks?

“Pieces can be gathered from many places and with many methods. What has always worked very well for me is the Lego Stores Pick-a-brick and consistently buying parts. Since Pick-a-brick rotates the pieces for purchase I always aim to buy in a usable quantity while I can. Plan ahead! Know what parts you are going to need for future builds so you can buy them well in advance when they are available and a good price. Due to ordering ahead of time, I currently have many of the parts needed for countless different builds that I have planned going years into the future.”

Keeping the design looking consistent and cohesive must be a challenge. What extent to do go to when planning a project on this scale?

“Have a plan for the project and know the style of the creation. I am lucky enough to build mainly repetitive large structures, but slight variation always helps so it doesnt look like the build has been ‘copied and pasted.’”
​
Do you have any unforeseen problems someone might run into with structure or just LEGO logistics on something similar? Do you have any interesting stories regarding that?

“Unforeseen problems have plagued my most recent, largest builds. Structure on smaller structures is often built for aesthetics.. but i learnt just how important it was when it came to large scale multi-floor buildings. My Felucia build ran into some massive structural failures and I was just lucky to get a video out of it. Then I moved on to my biggest creation, Tibrin. I created a new structural design that used the concrete and steel beams of lego aka Technic. I combined this with a layer of bricks for the floor above and plates the roof below and created a solid super structure. I later climbed inside the build and took out the supports and I then realized how much that structure was really supporting. -Last words of Kris
Time is a massive factor in large scale building. Ever put together a lego set and it took you over an hour? Now imagine, no instructions, only your head. How long is it going to take? That is just a regular set. I have spent weeks of building time on my creations and finding that time can be a struggle. “

Just to wrap it up, what are your biggest tips for building big?

“My tips for large scale building are:
-Know your pieces and how far they can go
-Know what you are building
-Plan far ahead
-Buy useful pieces in useful quantities
Key: Work towards the scale you want. If you can't build it now, start small and work up. I leave out lots having to do with skill, but you want the build to look good right? Skill comes with practice. Practice and work your way up to the projects of your dreams! Seriously. Anything is possible. You can't even imagine the things you could build! I mean.. I got eaten alive by one of my builds.. never would have imagined that..”
 
Builders don’t always find good opportunities after MOCs or even during their LEGO careers to share some of their best secrets to success with the rest of the MOC building community. I know sometimes I feel left wanting to know more about the process behind say a Nick Trotta Starfighter; I hope this interview gave that insight to my fellow FOLs out there. Thanks for reading and thanks for KrisProductions for giving an incredible interview!
Till the next post,

ï»¿Blog Kickstart!

​   Since Brickworld, RebelLUG hasn’t done too much in the eye of the public, although many exciting things are definitely in the works. In our recent meetings we’ve discussed how to make RebelLUG and its members more identifiable as a group. Our efforts right now will be aimed toward become a little more active as a group; along with future collaborative builds and more social media related ventures to come, the RebelLUG blog will start to become a lot more frequent.
   Starting this weekend and as many weeks after as I can muster, we’ll be trying to tell as interesting stories and useful articles as possible related to LEGO and RebelLUG. Don’t be afraid to comment ideas and criticism, as I’m trying to figure this blog out as we go and make it as entertaining as possible for the readers. If there’s something you want to know more about, whether that be a technique, a build, a member, making videos, etcetera, let me know!
    While there are loads of blogs about LEGO out there, I believe we can provide a unique perspective on the whole hobby. As a LUG of builders who are always working on something new and (hopefully) cool, we can try and provide some more of our experiences in the building scene, the social side, and everything inbetween. I only hope to reach the level of the KiethLUG blog in time, which I highly recommend taking a read on.
    With this, and the YouTube channel, RebelLUG is looking to share all our experiences and LEGO know-how with the community. There’s stock piles on the ready to be as soon as it comes to fruition, but if anything special of specific comes to mind for any member of the group, leave a comment and we can try and get to it.
   As my David Bowie playlist comes to a close, so does this entry blog post. Really what could make this blog special with help from the readers and for the readers is extra discussion down in the comments. There is endless potential for what we can talk about, as long as it gets brought up, so don’t hesitate. Anyway, I’m really hoping this blog and the future YouTube channel go to some real use for my fellow LEGO enthusiasts and the community as a whole.
Till the next post,
Spencer
